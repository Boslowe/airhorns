Important clarification on SP 11516

Earlier internal correspondence described SP 11516 as Jon's supplier's permit, implying exclusive ownership. That was imprecise. A DOT Special Permit is a federal regulatory authorization issued by PHMSA to a named Grantee company, with additional companies allowed to ship under that permit only if they have been granted Party Status by PHMSA. The legal violation by these competitors is not theft of "our" permit, it is unauthorized representation of compliance with a federal special permit they have not been granted authorization to use, on a product whose contents may not match the permit's authorized substances.

Track 1, EPA AIM Act, R 134a banned in aerosols effective January 1, 2025

Statutory authority: American Innovation and Manufacturing (AIM) Act of 2020, codified at 42 U.S.C. 7675.

Implementing regulations: 40 CFR Part 84 Subpart B ("Restrictions on the Use of Hydrofluorocarbons"), promulgated via the EPA Technology Transitions Final Rule, 88 FR 73098 (October 24, 2023).

The rule: Effective January 1, 2025, all aerosol products manufactured in or imported into the United States that use a regulated HFC substance with a global warming potential (GWP) greater than 150 are prohibited.

The violation: R 134a (1,1,1,2 Tetrafluoroethane, CAS# 811 97 2) has a 100 year GWP of 1,430 under IPCC Fourth Assessment Report (AR4) values, which is the GWP framework EPA uses for the Technology Transitions Rule. R 134a is therefore a prohibited propellant in any consumer aerosol manufactured or imported on or after January 1, 2025.

Evidence in this matter: 15 of 27 ASINs explicitly state "Contains: 1,1,1,2 Tetrafluoroethane" on the can label. 6 ASINs have manufacturing dates printed on the product after January 1, 2025, eliminating any pre ban inventory defense:

Track 2, Hazmat marking violation, undeclared shipping name and identification number

This is the cleanest of the three tracks. It does not depend on chemical analysis of the can contents.

49 CFR 172.301(a): "Except as otherwise provided by this subchapter, each person who offers a hazardous material for transportation in a non-bulk packaging must mark the package with the proper shipping name and identification number (preceded by 'UN', 'NA' or 'ID,' as appropriate), as shown in the 172.101 Hazardous Materials Table."
49 CFR 172.101 Hazardous Materials Table: For aerosols containing flammable propellants such as 1,1 Difluoroethane (R 152a) or 1,1,1,2 Tetrafluoroethane (R 134a), the proper shipping name is "Aerosols, flammable" or "Aerosols" with UN identification number UN1950. Federal hazmat regulations require this name and the UN1950 marking on the package.
49 CFR 171.2(e): "No person may offer or accept a hazardous material for transportation in commerce unless the hazardous material is properly classed, described, packaged, marked, labeled, and in condition for shipment as required or authorized by applicable requirements of this subchapter."

The violation: 11 of 27 ASINs in this matter omit the chemical identity entirely from the can label. The packaging shows generic warnings such as "compressed gas" or "Flammable Contents Under Pressure" but does not state R 134a, R 152a, any chemical name, or the UN1950 proper shipping name and identification number. This is a standalone hazmat marking violation under 49 CFR 172.301(a) and 49 CFR 171.2(e), separate from any AIM Act issue.

Practical implication for the takedown: even if Paul cannot prove the contents of any specific can (which would trigger the AIM Act), the failure to mark the proper shipping name and UN identification number on a regulated hazmat package is itself a violation of federal hazmat shipping requirements. Amazon's hazmat acceptance program at FBA inbound depends on these markings being present and accurate.

Track 3, Unauthorized use of DOT SP 11516

The regulatory structure: Per 49 CFR Part 107 Subpart B, DOT Special Permits (DOT SP) are individualized federal authorizations granted by PHMSA, not generic regulatory codes. Authorization to ship under any DOT SP number is restricted to:

  1. The named Grantee of the permit (the original applicant), and
  2. Any company granted Party Status by PHMSA via a specific Party To letter, issued upon application under 49 CFR 107.107.

Public verification: PHMSA maintains an authoritative searchable database of all DOT SP grantees and parties to at phmsa.dot.gov/approvals-and-permits/hazmat/special-permits-search.

The search results for SP 11516 are reproduced below. The PHMSA database was searched and confirmed there are 29 authorized entities total. None of the 16 Chinese or Hong Kong sellers in this matter appear on the list. Screenshots of all three pages of the PHMSA search results are in the evidence folder.

Track 4, False marking and misrepresentation under 49 CFR 171.2 (the strongest single legal vector)

49 CFR 171.2(g): "No person may represent, mark, certify, sell, or offer a packaging or container as meeting the requirements of an exemption, a special permit, approval, or registration issued under this subchapter or subchapter A of this chapter unless the packaging or container is manufactured, fabricated, marked, maintained, reconditioned, repaired, and retested in accordance with the applicable requirements of the exemption, special permit, approval, or registration issued under this subchapter or subchapter A of this chapter."
49 CFR 171.2(h)(2) expressly identifies "Exemption, special permit, approval, and registration numbers that include the letters 'DOT', 'EX', 'M', or 'R'" as the protected representations.
49 CFR 171.2(j): "No person may, by marking or otherwise, represent that a container or package for transportation of a hazardous material is safe, certified, or in compliance with the requirements of this chapter unless it meets the requirements of all applicable regulations issued under Federal hazardous material transportation law."
49 CFR 172.301(c) (the marking rule correlate): "Except as provided in 173.23 of this subchapter, the outside of each package authorized by a special permit must be plainly and durably marked 'DOT SP' followed by the special permit number assigned." By regulatory implication, the marking is reserved for packages actually authorized by the cited permit.

A competitor seller printing "DOT SP 11516" on a can of R 134a is making two simultaneous false representations:

  1. False representation of authorization. The seller has not been granted Grantee or Party To status for SP 11516 (confirmed via PHMSA database).
  2. False representation of substance compliance. SP 11516 authorizes specific hazmat substances. The contents of these cans (R 134a in 8 of 9 cases) do not match the authorized substance, and R 134a has been federally banned for this application as of January 1, 2025 in any case.

Civil penalties under 49 CFR 107.329 (current 2025 figures):

  • Knowing violation: up to $102,348 per violation, per day. Each day of continuing violation is a separate offense.
  • Knowing violation resulting in death, serious illness, severe injury, or substantial property destruction: up to $238,809 per violation, per day.
  • Criminal exposure: a knowing violation that results in serious injury or death may carry criminal penalties under 49 U.S.C. 5124 (up to 5 years imprisonment, or 10 years if the violation involves the release of hazardous material that results in death or bodily injury).

Track 5, FIFRA, unregistered pesticidal claims (7 U.S.C. 136 et seq.)

The Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA) regulates products that make claims to repel, deter, kill, or otherwise act on pests, including animals such as bears, coyotes, and dogs. Any product making such claims must be registered with the EPA and bear an EPA Establishment Number.

The violation: 25 of 27 ASINs in this matter make pesticidal claims (bear deterrent, dog deterrent, scare wildlife, stops barking, pet trainer to stop dog attacking, and similar) without an EPA Establishment Number visible in the listing or on the packaging. The Amazon listings themselves carry the violation, regardless of can contents.

Track 6, Country of origin marking

Under 19 U.S.C. 1304 and accompanying CBP regulations, imported goods must be marked with the country of origin in a conspicuous place. Marking solely on a removable Amazon shipping or inventory sticker does not satisfy the requirement when the underlying product packaging itself omits country of origin disclosure.

The violation: several ASINs in this matter omit country of origin from the permanent product packaging entirely, with the only Made in China indication appearing on a peel-off Amazon sticker.

Additional enforcement vectors

False USCG approval claims

B0FRMYBZBG (Innsky / Hong Kong) explicitly markets its product as "U.S. Coast Guard Approved for vessels up to 65 ft." USCG does not issue "approvals" for aerosol air horns. Compliant products meet SOLAS and 33 CFR 88.9 sound requirements but cannot claim USCG "approval." Likely present on additional ASINs.

Hazmat label forgery, contradictory safety markings

6 ASINs from the Sports & Outdoors Shop USA (Tianjin) cluster display a green Class 2.2 Non Flammable Gas hazmat diamond pictogram on the back of the can while the front says "FLAMMABLE CONTENTS UNDER PRESSURE." This is a direct contradiction between the formal DOT/GHS hazard class symbol and the safety text on the same product. Independently a violation of 49 CFR 172.400 and 49 CFR 172.401.
