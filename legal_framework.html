Important clarification on SP 11516

Earlier internal correspondence described SP 11516 as Jon's supplier's permit, implying exclusive ownership. That was imprecise. A DOT Special Permit is a federal regulatory authorization issued by PHMSA to a named Grantee company, with additional companies allowed to ship under that permit only if they have been granted Party Status by PHMSA. The legal violation by these competitors is not theft of "our" permit, it is unauthorized representation of compliance with a federal special permit they have not been granted authorization to use, on a product whose contents do not match the permit's authorized substances.

Strongest levers, ordered for counsel: The Tracks below are presented in order of evidentiary force. Track 1 (false marking under 49 CFR 171.2(g)) is the slam-dunk lever and requires no AIM Act argument, no chemical analysis, and no proof of intent. Track 2 (SP 11516 substance and authorization mismatch from PHMSA's primary documents) hardens Track 1 with the actual PHMSA grant text. Track 3 (Amazon's direct culpability under DOT-SP 21607) and Track 4 (Amazon's prior notice and ongoing constructive knowledge) put Amazon itself in PHMSA's enforcement crosshairs. Tracks 5 through 8 are independent supporting violations any one of which is on its own actionable.

Track 1, False marking and misrepresentation under 49 CFR 171.2 (the slam-dunk legal vector)

The single strongest argument in this matter, and the one that does not depend on chemical analysis, AIM Act sector scope, or any contested element of intent. Printing a federal special permit number on hazmat packaging without authorization is a standalone violation visible from the can label and verifiable against PHMSA's primary database.

49 CFR 171.2(g): "No person may represent, mark, certify, sell, or offer a packaging or container as meeting the requirements of an exemption, a special permit, approval, or registration issued under this subchapter or subchapter A of this chapter unless the packaging or container is manufactured, fabricated, marked, maintained, reconditioned, repaired, and retested in accordance with the applicable requirements of the exemption, special permit, approval, or registration issued under this subchapter or subchapter A of this chapter."
49 CFR 171.2(h)(2) expressly identifies "Exemption, special permit, approval, and registration numbers that include the letters 'DOT', 'EX', 'M', or 'R'" as the protected representations.
49 CFR 171.2(j): "No person may, by marking or otherwise, represent that a container or package for transportation of a hazardous material is safe, certified, or in compliance with the requirements of this chapter unless it meets the requirements of all applicable regulations issued under Federal hazardous material transportation law."
49 CFR 172.301(c) (the marking rule correlate): "Except as provided in 173.23 of this subchapter, the outside of each package authorized by a special permit must be plainly and durably marked 'DOT SP' followed by the special permit number assigned." By regulatory implication, the marking is reserved for packages actually authorized by the cited permit.

Two independent violations of 171.2(g) on these cans

A seller printing "DOT SP 11516" on a can of R-134a aerosol commits two simultaneous and independent false representations under 49 CFR 171.2(g):

  1. False representation of authorization (the authorized-party violation). None of the 16 Chinese or Hong Kong sellers in this matter appear in PHMSA's Special Permits database as Grantee or Party-To for SP 11516. The full 29-entity authorized list is reproduced in Track 2. Any one of these sellers printing SP 11516 on a can is unauthorized to apply that marking, regardless of can contents.
  2. False representation of substance compliance (the authorized-substance violation). SP 11516 authorizes a closed list of specific hazardous materials (paragraph 6 of the base permit text). R-134a as a pure substance is not on the SP 11516 authorized substances list at all. The marking "DOT SP 11516" on a can of R-134a is therefore a false representation of compliance with a permit that does not authorize that substance, regardless of who applied the marking and regardless of whether the supplier is on the authorized list.
Why this matters for the supply-chain defense. Two of the 29 SP 11516 authorized entities (Shanghai Zhantu Chemical and Conghua Trading) are based in Shanghai, raising a possible argument that the Chinese resellers in this matter are sourcing finished cans from these authorized upstream entities and reselling lawfully marked product. The substance-mismatch finding closes this defense entirely. Even assuming arguendo that all 9 SP 11516-marked ASINs were sourced from Zhantu or Conghua, R-134a is not on the SP 11516 authorized substances list, so neither Zhantu nor Conghua can lawfully apply SP 11516 to a can of R-134a. The marking is unauthorized at every level of the chain. Track 2 below quotes the relevant paragraph of the SP 11516 base permit text directly.

The hits on the existing record

  • 9 of 27 ASINs print "DOT SP 11516" on the can. None of the sellers are authorized parties (PHMSA database). 8 of 9 cans (where contents are stated) declare R-134a, which is not on SP 11516's authorized substance list. Both prongs of 171.2(g) are violated.
  • 4 of 27 ASINs print "DOT SP 21607" on the can (Amazon's own permit). None of the Chinese sellers are authorized parties. The authorized-party prong of 171.2(g) is violated. 2 of these 4 ASINs (B0D97CRVM2 and B0F62YLXK6) also explicitly declare R-134a on the same label, which is independent of SP 21607's substance scope and is the basis of Track 3 below.

Civil and criminal penalties under 49 CFR 107.329

  • Knowing violation: up to $102,348 per violation, per day. Each day of continuing violation is a separate offense.
  • Knowing violation resulting in death, serious illness, severe injury, or substantial property destruction: up to $238,809 per violation, per day.
  • Criminal exposure: a knowing violation that results in serious injury or death may carry criminal penalties under 49 U.S.C. 5124 (up to 5 years imprisonment, or 10 years if the violation involves the release of hazardous material that results in death or bodily injury).

Track 2, DOT SP 11516, authorized parties and authorized substances (the foundation for Track 1)

This Track documents the two facts that make Track 1 bulletproof: (a) PHMSA's authorized parties list for SP 11516, and (b) the closed list of substances actually authorized under the SP 11516 base permit text.

The regulatory structure

Per 49 CFR Part 107 Subpart B, DOT Special Permits (DOT SP) are individualized federal authorizations granted by PHMSA, not generic regulatory codes. Authorization to ship under any DOT SP number is restricted to:

  1. The named Grantee of the permit (the original applicant), and
  2. Any company granted Party Status by PHMSA via a specific Party-To letter, issued upon application under 49 CFR 107.107.

Public verification: PHMSA maintains an authoritative searchable database of all DOT SP grantees and parties-to at phmsa.dot.gov/approvals-and-permits/hazmat/special-permits-search.

The reseller carve-out at paragraph 8(a) does not save the Chinese sellers

49 CFR Part 171 / SP 11516 paragraph 8(a): "A person who is not a holder of this special permit who receives a package covered by this special permit may reoffer it for transportation provided no modification or change is made to the package or its contents and it is reoffered for transportation in conformance with this special permit and the HMR."

This carve-out exists to allow downstream handlers to move lawfully-permitted packages without applying for their own party-to status. It requires two conditions, both of which fail here:

  1. The package must be covered by the permit. A can of R-134a is not covered by SP 11516 at all because R-134a is not on the authorized substance list. The carve-out cannot apply to packages that were never within the permit's scope.
  2. No modification or change to the package or its contents. Each Chinese seller in this matter rebrands the product with their own listing copy, brand name, and (in most cases) outer packaging. That is modification within the meaning of paragraph 8(a). The carve-out is voided once a downstream handler rebrands or repackages.

Paragraph 8(b) additionally requires the permit document itself to be maintained at every facility offering or reoffering the package; the Chinese sellers do not maintain SP 11516 documentation as a matter of compliance practice and are not entities of record with PHMSA.

Key permit language (from the actual SP 21607 grant text)

The official PHMSA permit text is in the SP21607_permit_text.pdf on this site. Two operative provisions are decisive:

On scope (Section 2.a of the permit):
"This special permit authorizes the transportation in commerce of packages containing limited quantities of hazardous materials with a reduced size limited quantity marking that is placed on the package tracking label, and without shipping papers under the conditions noted herein. This special permit provides no relief from the Hazardous Materials Regulations (HMR) other than as specifically stated. The most recent revision supersedes all previous revisions."

SP 21607 is purely a labeling and shipping-papers relief permit. It is logistical, not substantive. It does not authorize the transportation of any substance that is otherwise prohibited under the HMR or other federal law (such as the EPA Technology Transitions Final Rule prohibition on R-134a in consumer aerosols). Amazon's authorization under SP 21607 does not extend permission to transport prohibited hazmat.

On who may be a party-to (Section 2.d of the permit):
"Party status to this special permit may only be granted to third party affiliates of Amazon.com, Inc. that utilize Amazon's hazardous materials classification system and customer fulfillment technologies in the Amazon Fulfillment Network."

None of the Chinese sellers in this matter appear in the PHMSA Special Permits database as Grantee or Party-To for SP 21607. The permit text restricts Party-To eligibility to "third party affiliates of Amazon.com, Inc. that utilize Amazon's hazardous materials classification system and customer fulfillment technologies in the Amazon Fulfillment Network" (Section 2.d, quoted above). The sellers in this matter are independent third-party sellers using Amazon as a fulfillment vendor, not Amazon affiliates using Amazon's hazmat classification system. Their printing of "DOT-SP 21607" on the can is therefore an unauthorized representation of compliance with a federal special permit they have not been granted authorization to use, in violation of 49 CFR 171.2(g) regardless of who fulfills the shipment.

The actual signed Amazon grant letter (tracking number 2025075004, granted August 14, 2025, expires May 31, 2029) is in the SP21607_amazon_grant_letter.pdf on this site for reference.

Why this exposes Amazon to direct PHMSA enforcement

49 CFR 171.2(f): "No person may transport a hazardous material in commerce unless the hazardous material is transported in accordance with applicable requirements of this subchapter, or an exemption or special permit, approval, or registration issued under this subchapter or subchapter A of this chapter. Each carrier who transports a hazardous material in commerce may rely on information provided by the offeror of the hazardous material or a prior carrier, unless the carrier knows or, a reasonable person, acting in the circumstances and exercising reasonable care, would have knowledge that the information provided by the offeror or prior carrier is incorrect."

Amazon, as the SP 21607 grantee, is the federally authorized transporter of these packages in US commerce. Under the FBA model Amazon also performs functions of an offeror under 49 CFR 171.2(a) (classification, description, and packaging certification for transportation), creating dual offeror and transporter responsibilities on shipments fulfilled through FBA. The Chinese seller's printing of SP 21607 on the can effectively claims Amazon's permit authority. When Amazon then fulfills the order through FBA, Amazon is the entity physically transporting the package under its own permit number.

Amazon's reasonable-carrier reliance defense under 49 CFR 171.2(f) applies only until Amazon has actual or constructive knowledge that the information provided by the offeror is incorrect. This filing constitutes such notice; the chronology in Track 4 establishes that Amazon also had documented constructive knowledge from prior reports going back to November 2024. Once Amazon has notice that specific ASINs in their FBA program contain R-134a (prohibited in the consumer aerosol end-use sector under EPA's Technology Transitions Final Rule effective January 1, 2025) and are marked with Amazon's own SP 21607 permit, continued FBA fulfillment is no longer covered by the reliance defense. Continued fulfillment after notice creates substantial risk of being treated as a knowing violation, with Amazon transporting non-compliant hazmat under its own federal authorization.

Civil penalties under 49 CFR 107.329 (2025 inflation-adjusted figures) for knowing violations: up to $102,348 per violation per day, or up to $238,809 per violation per day if injury or substantial property destruction results. Each day of continuing violation constitutes a separate offense.

Supporting precedent

In March 2026, the Washington State Department of Ecology obtained civil penalties against Amazon and Walmart for selling banned refrigerants through their platforms. (Reference: Washington State Standard, March 2026.) Federal enforcement under PHMSA jurisdiction is more direct because Amazon is the actual federally-authorized transporter, not merely the seller of record.

Practical implication for the takedown

This argument repositions the matter from "please remove these listings" to "Amazon, you have direct federal exposure on these specific ASINs and need to act to limit it." Amazon's incentive to act fast is now grounded in its own liability rather than in cooperation with the complainant. Prior submissions through Amazon's standard hazmat reporting channel were ineffective; this filing should be directed to Amazon's legal department or hazmat compliance team with explicit reference to 49 CFR 171.2(f).

Why this matters legally

Per 49 CFR 171.2(f), a carrier may rely on information from the offeror only "unless the carrier knows or, a reasonable person, acting in the circumstances and exercising reasonable care, would have knowledge that the information provided by the offeror or prior carrier is incorrect."

Amazon's RISC (Restricted Products) team and Senior Managers received documented written reports identifying these specific seller categories and the R-134a issue starting February 2025, with formal in-person submission to the Restricted Products team in September 2025. On the existing record, Amazon's reasonable-carrier reliance defense under 49 CFR 171.2(f) is substantially compromised retroactive to those notice dates. Continued FBA fulfillment of these ASINs after those dates supports a finding of knowing violation under 49 CFR 107.329, carrying penalties up to $102,348 per violation per day, $238,809 if injury results, with each day a separate offense.

The reporting client also notes that in earlier communications to Amazon, the legal theory was framed as a "bulk HFC importer list violation under the AIM Act." That framing was imprecise. The bulk-importer list provision applies to bulk refrigerant, not finished consumer aerosols. The accurate legal theory is the EPA Technology Transitions Final Rule (40 CFR Part 84 Subpart B), the unauthorized DOT special permit markings under 49 CFR 171.2(g), and Amazon's own SP 21607 culpability under 49 CFR 171.2(f). The legal accuracy of the theory does not depend on how it was originally communicated to Amazon. The notice element under 171.2(f) only requires that Amazon, exercising reasonable care, would have known the underlying information was incorrect. The substantive content of the prior submissions (R-134a aerosols sold post-ban, FIFRA pesticidal claims, missing country-of-origin, missing hazmat warning labels) was sufficient to put a reasonable carrier on inquiry notice.

Track 5, EPA AIM Act, R 134a prohibited in consumer aerosols effective January 1, 2025

Statutory authority: American Innovation and Manufacturing (AIM) Act of 2020, codified at 42 U.S.C. 7675.

Implementing regulations: 40 CFR Part 84 Subpart B ("Restrictions on the Use of Hydrofluorocarbons"), promulgated via the EPA Technology Transitions Final Rule, 88 FR 73098 (October 24, 2023).

The rule: Effective January 1, 2025, the EPA Technology Transitions Final Rule prohibits the manufacture or import of aerosol products in the regulated end-use sectors (including the consumer aerosols sector) that use a regulated HFC with a global warming potential (GWP) greater than 150. Air horns are consumer aerosol products squarely within the regulated end-use sector covered by the rule.

The violation: R 134a (1,1,1,2 Tetrafluoroethane, CAS# 811 97 2) has a 100 year GWP of 1,430 under IPCC Fourth Assessment Report (AR4) values, the GWP framework EPA uses for the Technology Transitions Rule. R 134a is therefore a prohibited propellant in consumer aerosol products manufactured or imported on or after January 1, 2025.

Evidence in this matter: 15 of 27 ASINs explicitly state "Contains: 1,1,1,2 Tetrafluoroethane" on the can label. 6 ASINs have manufacturing dates printed on the product after January 1, 2025, eliminating any pre ban inventory defense:

Track 6, Hazmat marking violation, undeclared shipping name and identification number

An independent standalone violation. Does not depend on chemical analysis of the can contents, the AIM Act argument, or any party-to question.

49 CFR 172.301(a): "Except as otherwise provided by this subchapter, each person who offers a hazardous material for transportation in a non-bulk packaging must mark the package with the proper shipping name and identification number (preceded by 'UN', 'NA' or 'ID,' as appropriate), as shown in the 172.101 Hazardous Materials Table."
49 CFR 172.101 Hazardous Materials Table: For aerosols containing flammable propellants such as 1,1 Difluoroethane (R 152a) or 1,1,1,2 Tetrafluoroethane (R 134a), the proper shipping name is "Aerosols, flammable" or "Aerosols" with UN identification number UN1950. Federal hazmat regulations require this name and the UN1950 marking on the package.
49 CFR 171.2(e): "No person may offer or accept a hazardous material for transportation in commerce unless the hazardous material is properly classed, described, packaged, marked, labeled, and in condition for shipment as required or authorized by applicable requirements of this subchapter."

The violation: 11 of 27 ASINs in this matter omit the chemical identity entirely from the can label. The packaging shows generic warnings such as "compressed gas" or "Flammable Contents Under Pressure" but does not state R 134a, R 152a, any chemical name, or the UN1950 proper shipping name and identification number on the can.

Note on the Limited Quantity exception: under 49 CFR 173.156, hazardous materials shipped as Limited Quantity may use the Ltd Qty diamond on the outer carton in lieu of the full proper shipping name and UN ID on that outer package. The Ltd Qty exception does not, however, eliminate the marking obligations on the inner packaging (the can itself), and the Ltd Qty exception is contingent on compliance with all other applicable HMR requirements, including any special permit being claimed. Where these sellers print "DOT SP 11516" on the can but are not authorized parties to that permit, the Ltd Qty pathway is not available to them, and the standalone marking violation under 49 CFR 172.301(a) and 49 CFR 171.2(e) on the inner packaging stands.

Practical implication for the takedown: even where the contents of a specific can cannot be proven (which would trigger the AIM Act), the failure to mark the proper shipping name and UN identification number on the inner packaging of a regulated hazmat product is itself a violation of federal hazmat shipping requirements. Amazon's hazmat acceptance program at FBA inbound depends on these markings being present and accurate.

Track 7, FIFRA, unregistered pesticidal claims (7 U.S.C. 136 et seq.)

The Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA) regulates substances and devices intended to prevent, destroy, repel, or mitigate pests. Where a product is marketed with explicit pesticidal claims (repel, deter, kill, or otherwise act on a pest), the product is generally subject to FIFRA registration or, in the case of devices, the establishment registration and labeling requirements of 40 CFR Part 152 Subpart I (devices) and Part 167 (establishments). Mechanical scare devices framed without pesticidal effect claims fall in a more nuanced area; the violation here turns on the explicit pesticidal language used in the listings.

The violation: 25 of 27 ASINs in this matter use explicit pesticidal claims in the listing copy itself ("bear deterrent," "dog deterrent," "stops dog attacks," "scare wildlife," "stops barking," "pet trainer," and similar) without an EPA Establishment Number visible in the listing or on the packaging. Products marketed with explicit pesticidal claims of this kind are subject to FIFRA registration and labeling requirements; the absence of any EPA Establishment Number on these listings is the violation. The listings themselves carry the violation, regardless of can contents.

Track 8, Country of origin marking

Under 19 U.S.C. 1304 and accompanying CBP regulations, imported goods must be marked with the country of origin in a conspicuous place. Marking solely on a removable Amazon shipping or inventory sticker does not satisfy the requirement when the underlying product packaging itself omits country of origin disclosure.

The violation: several ASINs in this matter omit country of origin from the permanent product packaging entirely, with the only Made in China indication appearing on a peel-off Amazon sticker.

Additional enforcement vectors

False USCG approval claims

B0FRMYBZBG (Innsky / Hong Kong) explicitly markets its product as "U.S. Coast Guard Approved for vessels up to 65 ft." The USCG does not issue product approvals for consumer aerosol air horns. Type Approval under 46 CFR Subchapter Q applies to performance characteristics for vessel sound-signaling appliances on inspected vessels, not to the aerosol canister itself. Compliant products meet the sound requirements of 33 CFR 83.33 (Inland Rules) and Annex III of the COLREGs but cannot claim USCG "approval." The marketing claim therefore is a false representation of federal certification, with potential exposure under 15 U.S.C. 1125 (Lanham Act) for false designation of origin. Likely present on additional ASINs.

Hazmat label forgery, contradictory safety markings

6 ASINs from the Sports & Outdoors Shop USA (Tianjin) cluster display a green Class 2.2 Non Flammable Gas hazmat diamond pictogram on the back of the can while the front says "FLAMMABLE CONTENTS UNDER PRESSURE." The formal DOT/GHS hazard class symbol and the safety text on the same product directly contradict each other, suggesting either misclassification or inconsistent hazard communication independently in violation of 49 CFR 172.400 and 49 CFR 172.401.
